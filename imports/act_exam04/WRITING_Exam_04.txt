WRITING Exam 4
PART A � WRITING PROMPT
W4-001
Prompt:
As schools look for ways to prepare students for life after graduation, many are reconsidering what kinds of experiences should count as essential education. Some people argue that every student should complete a real-world learning experience�such as community service, an internship, or a local project�before graduating. They believe that these experiences help students connect classroom learning to adult responsibilities. Others argue that graduation requirements are already demanding enough and that adding another requirement would create unfair pressure, especially for students with jobs, family responsibilities, or limited transportation. Still others believe such experiences can be valuable, but only if schools provide enough flexibility and support to make them realistic for all students.
Issue:
Should high schools require students to complete a real-world learning experience, such as community service, an internship, or a community project, in order to graduate?
Perspective 1:
High schools should require a real-world learning experience because students need structured opportunities to apply what they learn, build responsibility, and engage with the world beyond the classroom.
Perspective 2:
High schools should not require a real-world learning experience because graduation should be based mainly on academic achievement, and additional requirements may unfairly burden some students.
Perspective 3:
High schools may require a real-world learning experience, but only if they offer flexible options and enough support to ensure that the requirement is meaningful and fair.
Essay Task:
Write a unified, coherent essay in which you:
* clearly state your own perspective on the issue
* analyze the relationship between your perspective and at least 1 other perspective
* develop your ideas with logical reasoning and specific examples
* organize your ideas clearly
* use precise language and correct grammar and punctuation
PART B � SCORING GUIDE
Scoring Focus:
* Ideas and Analysis
* Development and Support
* Organization
* Language Use and Conventions
* Ideas and Analysis: A strong response presents a clear perspective on the issue, addresses its complexity, and explains how that perspective relates to at least 1 other perspective.
* Development and Support: A strong response develops its argument with relevant reasoning and specific examples rather than relying on general statements alone.
* Organization: A strong response is logically structured, with a focused introduction, well-connected body paragraphs, and a conclusion that reinforces the main argument.
* Language Use and Conventions: A strong response uses precise word choice, varied sentence structure, and generally correct grammar, usage, and punctuation.
